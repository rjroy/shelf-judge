**Meeting Notes: Shelf Judge Vision Definition**

**Summary**

The session established the foundational vision and principles for shelf-judge, a board game collection tool designed to help collectors understand what their shelf says about them and make intentional decisions about acquisitions and curation. The user opened with an elevator pitch describing the core capability: recording games with user-provided and BGG-sourced stats, calculating a singular fitness value, and predicting fitness for unowned games. Celeste reframed the discussion away from features toward underlying principles, identifying that "ownership is personal and specific" as the core insight—games earn their place for reasons unique to the owner (mechanics, art, theme, social context, sentiment), and the system must treat multi-axis ratings as first-class concerns, not decorations.

Five guiding principles were defined: (1) Ownership is personal and specific, driving multi-axis user-defined ratings; (2) One number, honestly derived, requiring transparent breakdowns of how fitness scores are calculated; (3) Your collection has an identity, making preference profiles legible to owners; (4) Data serves judgment, not replaces it, positioning BGG data as context rather than authority; and (5) The shelf has a carrying capacity, meaning fitness is relative and redundancy matters. Three anti-goals were also articulated: the system does not make automated purchase decisions, does not include social/competitive features, and does not compete with BGG as a game database.

**Key Decisions**

The team prioritized principles over features as the north star, establishing that multi-axis, user-defined ratings are central to the design rather than a secondary feature. Fitness must always be interrogable and transparent; a score the user cannot understand is worthless. Redundancy detection—how adding a game affects the fitness of existing games—was accepted as a design consideration, though deferred in implementation until sufficient data exists to make the signal meaningful. The system was positioned as a curation tool for personal use, not a social platform or purchasing oracle.

**Artifacts Produced**

`.lore/vision.md` created as a draft, containing the vision statement, five principles with "looks like/doesn't look like" examples for each, three anti-goals, and a tension resolution table mapping conflicting design priorities to decision defaults (e.g., personal axes override BGG data accuracy; transparency always wins over precision).

**Follow-ups**

The next conversation should tackle the fitness score specification, where multi-axis ratings meet the actual mathematics. That design determines how personal axes feed into both fitness calculation and prediction. Until the fitness model is specified, subsequent architectural decisions cannot be made with confidence.
